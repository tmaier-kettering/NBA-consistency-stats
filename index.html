Consistency Explained

How can we measure consistency statistically?

Averages are useful, but they only tell part of the story. Two players can average the same points while delivering very different game-to-game results. This page explains why variability matters and how Consistency Rating (CR) captures reliability.

Explore the full data table

The average is only half the story

If one player scores near 20 every night and another alternates between very low and very high totals, their averages can match even though their consistency is clearly different.

Why variability matters

Standard deviation measures spread around the average. Low spread means steadier performance, high spread means more fluctuation.

Consistency Rating (CR)

CR adjusts variation relative to a player’s output, so consistency can be compared more fairly.

Equation
CR = Average ÷ Standard Deviation

Higher CR means performance is more tightly clustered around the player’s norm.

DeMar DeRozan and Scottie Barnes both scored roughly 18.5 points per game. However, DeRozan varied by nearly 9 points from game to game, while Barnes varied by only 6 points. This results in a clear picture of how consistent DeRozan and Barnes are. Finally, we can quantify this with the Consistency Rating (CR) for both players. Because Barnes has a smaller spread of points per game, his CR is higher, indicating a greater degree of consistency.
